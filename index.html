Today I would like to talk to you about a topic that is gaining increasing importance in the digital world: Malware. Malware stands for "Malicious software" and encompasses various types of harmful programs designed to cause damage to computers or networks.


1. Virus

A virus is a type of malware that attaches itself to existing programs or files and reproduces by embedding itself in other files. Viruses often spread through infected files, email attachments, or infected websites. They can damage or destroy data and impair the performance of a computer.


2. Trojan:

A Trojan is a disguised malware that pretends to be useful software to gain access to a system. Unlike viruses, Trojans cannot replicate themselves but require user interaction to be installed. Once activated, they can steal confidential information, open backdoors for attackers, or perform other malicious activities.


3. Worms:

Unlike viruses, worms spread independently without human intervention. They often exploit security vulnerabilities in networks to propagate. Worms can spread rapidly and cause significant harm by overloading network resources or stealing sensitive information.


To protect against these threats, it is important to raise security awareness and take appropriate protective measures. Regular updates, antivirus programs, and avoiding insecure websites and email attachments are basic practices to minimize the risk of malware.

In an increasingly interconnected world, understanding malware and implementing security measures are essential to protect our digital activities.


These hacker attacks are crucial for programmers!



1. SQL Injection:

SQL Injection refers to an attack technique where an attacker injects malicious SQL commands into an application, often through manipulated user inputs. This allows the attacker to access sensitive database contents.

2. Cross-Site Scripting (XSS):

XSS occurs when an attacker inserts malicious code into a webpage, which is then executed in a user's browser. This allows the attacker to steal user data or take over sessions.

3. Denial of Service (DoS) and Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS):

DoS and DDoS aim to overload a server's resources to prevent service for legitimate users.
